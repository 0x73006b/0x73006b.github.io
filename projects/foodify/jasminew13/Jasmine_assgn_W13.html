Evaluation and Analysis Results

A. Topic: Our team chose to explore the Save-On-Foods Interface, which allows a user to create a grocery list based on recipes or menus provided from a limited number of sources.

B. Evaluation Goals: The study evaluation aimed to address the following three questions regarding the interface’s efficiency, customizability and layout/aesthetic design:

  • Efficiency: How easily can the user navigate through the application to complete a task? An efficient interface helps reduce user frustrations with an application as they can accomplish their goals faster and with less effort.
  • Customizability: How personalized can the experience be without overloading the interface? Can users adequately personalize their experience to account for safety (ie. allergies and other dietary restrictions) and personal taste? Users should be able to tailor the app (to an extent) to their personal tastes and account for health needs
  • Layout/Aesthetics Design: Are the aesthetic design choices and layout of the interface pleasing and convenient for use? The functionalities of the app need to be discoverable through the chosen layout and use of signifiers.

C. Summary of Evaluation 

7 undergraduate students from UBC were recruited; they all fit the initial description of a university student with online shopping experience (purchased online within 6 months), though each had varying levels of expertise. Participant ages ranged from 19 to 25 and came from a variety of educational backgrounds, including psychology, computer science, biology, and marketing. The conceptual model used for the interface prototype was Spotify. 71% (5 of 7) of participants is a current or past user of Spotify, therefore transfer effects were expected and leveraged in the design. A medium fidelity prototype was built using Figma (Appendix A.1) and other evaluation instruments include a coding sheet (Appendix A.2) for standardized information recording, and a timer. One researcher was present to conduct and record observations for each of the 7 studies.

The study was conducted through the means of a controlled observation using a think-aloud method, followed by a semi-structured interview. The study was done in a quiet environment to ensure comfort for the users and ease of conversation during the interview. After reading and signing a consent form, participants were asked to use the prototype to complete two central tasks. A timer was set by the researcher to record how long it took for the participant to complete each individual task. Task 1 was to sign up and create a new profile for a lactose intolerant user, convert a recipe URL to a grocery list and then change quantities of the grocery list based on the availability of ingredients already at home. Task 2 involved searching for already existing recipes and filtering the results based on meal-type, price, and cuisine.

As participants were asked to use the think-aloud method throughout the study, notes were taken on user behavior, comments, and any critical incidents that occurred. Following the observation, participants were asked questions in a semi-structured interview to further gauge user satisfaction as well as to collect data in order to measure the evaluation goals. This data was extracted through qualitative coding aided by an affinity diagram (Appendix A.3). Quantitative data was evaluated by calculating the mean, median, and mode on a spreadsheet (Chart 1, Appendix A.3).

D. Evaluation Rationale 

Our evaluation aimed to understand how efficient, customizable, and aesthetically pleasing the interface is for users. These three goals were selected as the Save-On Foods interface is meant to save shoppers time buying groceries (efficiency). It should account for the diversity of users (customizability), such as those with allergies or who prefer certain cuisine types. Users should be able to access an easy to use, pleasant interface (layout and aesthetic design). Having a well-laid out interface can also contribute to the overall efficiency of the application, as users can spend less time trying to find certain buttons or functionalities. To properly evaluate these goals given our time and resource constraints, we chose to conduct a controlled observation using a think-aloud method, followed by a semi-structured interview. These methods were selected because it provided triangulation through the qualitative, quantitative, objective and subjective collection of data.

Efficiency was evaluated through both the observation and interview. Quantitative data (time for each task, efficiency rating, number of relevant critical incidents) and qualitative data (thoughts while completing tasks, comments during interview questions) were collected. Through the think-aloud observation, we were able to note where participants got stuck and pinpoint their frustrations based on which step of the task they were on. The think-aloud method was particularly useful as the user was able to talk through what was frustrating.

Customizability was also evaluated through both observation and interview. Quantitative data included personalization ratings on a Likert scale for specific interface features and qualitative data was collected through observation and interview. The think aloud observation method was especially useful in identifying participants customizability needs and the follow-up interview allowed participants to expand on these thoughts.

Finally, design aesthetic/layout was evaluated through qualitative measures (comments during the observation and specific interview questions regarding layout/button placement, color scheme and icons). Given time constraints, interview questions were the best choice for evaluating design aesthetic/layout, as user preferences tend to be subjective and not always explicit. Additionally, aesthetics may not be something a user consciously thinks about while using an application. Open-ended questions allowed for further probing helping us better evaluate this goal.

E. Prototyping Rationale

We limited our scope to what we identified as the tasks users would want to complete using the interface: creating recipes from other sources and browsing existing recipes in the database to get corresponding grocery lists. The functionality of our prototype that allowed us to test this included users creating a profile with a food allergy (walkthrough video, 0:25-0:54), searching/filtering existing recipes (1:20-1:42), substituting items based on allergies (1:43-1:50), reviewing grocery list items compared with original recipe (1:50-1:59), changing quantities (2:04-2:08), reviewing the shopping cart (2:18-2:27) and checking out (2:27-2:50) , finalizing payment (2:50-3:05), and inputting a URL to create a recipe (3:06-3:36).

Because our evaluation goals aimed to evaluate efficiency, layout aesthetic and customizability, our prototype has a depth focus (vertical scoping). Specifically, we wanted to test the end-to-end process of users finding/creating grocery lists from recipes, rather than the breadth of all potential interface features. For example, in our grocery list review step, users could only change the quantity of  “Eggs” and “Vanilla Bean”, (2:06, 2:42). However, there was some horizontal scoping to the extent that it applied to main tasks; we featured a user sign-up page (0:29-0:49) where users fill out their food allergies. Doing so allowed us to focus more on how users experienced the overall interface flow, and answer our evaluation goals with more detail. Limited horizontal scoping allowed testing user flows and evaluation of our areas of interest.

F. Summary of Data, Findings and Analysis

Participants were pleased with the overall experience, with an average satisfaction rating of 84% (4.2/5 average rating) (Figure 3). Many users enjoyed the concept of generating a grocery list based off recipes and noted the similarity between the prototype and Spotify’s interface design.

Participants evaluation of efficiency was extremely positive, with an average 96% (4.8/5) satisfaction rating (Figure 4). We found that in general, the task completion times were quick and can largely be attributed to transfer effects of both online shopping interfaces (checkout user flows) and the Spotify interface (which 71% of participants had used before). Participants explained that the process has a “smooth flow” and is “easy to use”. Inefficiencies included annoyance at scrolling down the ingredients list which was described as “[taking] too long”. As well, the sub-task of selecting items and their quantities within the grocery list was less satisfactory with a rating of 73% (3.64/5) (Figure 5). A small majority (4/7) of the participants expressed annoyance at having to press too many buttons to get to the next screen/confirm their choice (filters pop-up & done button). On average, converting a URL to a grocery list and checking out (Task 1), was completed in 2:46 minutes. An outlier was identified however, whose Task 1 time was 6:24 minutes. Without said outlier, the total average time taken for Task 1 is 2:10 minutes. Task 2, where participants were asked to search and filter through the existing recipe database, was completed in 44 seconds on average. Paired with the high satisfaction rating, these results indicate that, for the most part, the process of creating a grocery list from a URL and searching for a specific recipe are both efficient processes.

All participants were satisfied with the level of customizability the interface provided. Starting from app sign-in, participants mentioned that they appreciated the initial user quiz and the automatic food allergy/condition reminders pop-up that would prompt the selection of an alternative. The ability to choose payment options, as well as modify and confirm your grocery list at checkout, was also noted as a customizability strength by participants. That being said, participants also had many suggestions (elaborated below in Part H) such as adding more filtering options, being able to customize recipes with other ingredients, save modified recipes, and allow users to interact with each other in-app (share tips for recipes, comments, etc). However, these suggestions were mostly additional features instead of improvements, further solidifying participants’ satisfaction with the customizability that was already available in our prototype.

Through the evaluation results we found that many of the issues participants had with the interface were related to layout and signifiers for affordances. Issues that participants identified were lack of signifiers for certain affordances such as swipe to remove an item in a grocery list/shoppo]ing cart review. This was stated on top of the screen but was not visible to users who have scrolled down the list. We attempted to use a positive transfer effect for this affordance from general mobile app trends (Spotify swipe to remove song). However, from participant observation, this affordance was not transferred for any participant. Additionally, 2 users didn’t know to scroll to down the grocery list. But due to the limited sample size, it’s not clear if this is a signifier issue or a fault of the figma interface as a scroll bar was not present in the prototype. The largest number of critical incidents and suggestions for improvement occurred in the modifying quantities and checkout screens. While modifying quantities, there were no signifiers besides some text at the top of the page to indicate that the item could be tapped on to increase or decrease quantity. Many participants missed this, and were confused. At checkout there were two avenues for modifying a grocery list: a button at the top of the page and the checkout button at the bottom. This also confused users, as they would hesitate on which one to pick. Others accidently pressed the continue shopping button instead. Many users also requested the filter options be laid out under the search bar instead of as a separate button and screen.

G. Conclusions

We found that our design successfully meets our evaluation goals and user expectations of efficiency, customizability, and layout & aesthetic design. Participants gave an average 83% rating for their overall satisfaction with the two tasks they were asked to complete. Within this, the level of customizability and efficiency provided by the interface was rated 100% and 95%, respectively. Users overall impressions of the interface layout, flows, and aesthetic choices such as colour were well-regarded by participants, who enjoyed the design’s color scheme and noted the “smooth flow” and “[ease] of use”.

The initial ingredient list and the checkout user flow were the two sections where the most critical incidents occurred (44% and 22% of all Task 1 critical incidents). The first problem area at the initial and final grocery lists was found to be due to lack of signifiers for changing quantities and removing items. The second problem area during the checkout phase of the task was due to the overload of buttons that could have been pressed in order to achieve the same/similar looking results (checkout button vs. view entire grocery list button). This caused confusion, as a few participants failed to press the correct buttons. In Task 2, most problems occurred while the participant attempted to filter their search which came as a surprise to the team since it seemed relatively straightforward. Filtering existed as a separate button and therefore separate screen, causing annoyance as well as confusion. We realized this is likely due to a negative transfer effect for filtering option placement which is usually located directly on the search screen than as a separate pop-up.

H) Design Recommendations  

Our interface design for the medium-fidelity prototype combined the best aspects of our two initial paper prototypes. From participant feedback and evaluation results, we were able to achieve grocery list creation efficiency and customization, while maintaining a well laid out and aesthetically pleasing interface design. Comments from the participants showed that the aesthetic choices such as color scheme and layout were modern, edgy, and overall, well-designed.

As our prototype is based on a conceptual model that is already a successful mobile application, many users found the interface easy and efficient to navigate, due to the familiarity of Spotify’s interface. In concept, URL to recipe conversion was very efficient on the client side and participants were satisfied in its general ease of use and user flow. Users appreciated many aspects of the prototype that were targeted towards customizability, such as different payment and delivery options, prompts for allergy alternatives, and filter options.

From our evaluation, we found that there are problems with how the ingredients list and checkout was laid out. On the ingredient selection page, the process made sense to most users in regards to general workflow, however, better, more intuitive signifiers are required for users to efficiently remove and update items from the ingredients list. Users were confused when selecting ingredients from the list, with half noting the lack of numbers and quantities to be the cause. Participants were surprised that tapping removed the item completely. However, this could be attributed to the constraints of using figma to build our prototype. One participant noted swiping to be error prone. Suggestions to keep items crossed out so it is still visible on the list or to have an undo button are minor adjustments that can be addressed in the next design step.

Other minor adjustments to take into consideration is to increase overall efficiency and further improve layout designs. Some participants felt the grocery list took too long to scroll through and would prefer to see a more condensed list, with each ingredient having a corresponding picture and quantity. Users felt the filter functionality should have more visibility, perhaps with its own icon in the nav bar, and unnecessary, double confirmation dialogs should be removed. A quick checkout option that allows the user to directly add a recipe to the shopping cart, bypassing allergy checks and the updating of ingredients is worth investigating for future designs to save the user time. Many participants gave valuable feedback regarding future scalability and feature suggestions, such as Foodify Premium, third-party integration, and coupons. However, as those suggestions are beyond our scope, the approach will not be validated.

I) Critique of Process 

Over the last 12 weeks we’ve learned a lot about the importance of re-iteration informed by feedback (evaluations, heuristic evaluation, cognitive walkthroughs, etc) in user-centered design. Going through feedback and evaluation results together as a team helped us understand aspects of our current interface that we would not have thought about had we not first iterated on the design through in-team evaluations as well as involved participants through evaluation studies. This final project also allowed us to combine and apply a lot of the theory we’ve learned throughout the term, from transfer effects to considerations of signifiers in our design.

The methods used for the evaluation did the job well given our time and resource constraints. We were able to cover most of our bases through the observation and interview, however, in hindsight and given we had more time, we would have changed a few things in our design and evaluation process. First, we would have expanded our recruitment pool and talked to relevant stakeholders beforehand to determine user needs right off the bat. This study also only used college age students, however Save-On foods has many customers, including parents and elderly which our current design may not cater well to. Furthermore, only 7 observations were collected, in the future we would recruit more participants. Layout placement and use of buttons was the source of most confusion in our current design and could have been improved. A/B testing is one option that would help us effectively narrow down the best signifiers to use in the future.

Due to the team’s lack of previous prototyping skills with Figma, our med-fi prototype had some tool based constraints. This made it hard to properly implement some features such as a slider on the filter page. The low-fidelity, paper prototype was the most valuable and memorable activity we tried in designing our product. Having to split into sub-groups to create two different prototypes resulted in very useful divergences of ideas, ultimately leading to a better final prototype. Nothing in particular stood out as being of low-value in the design process.
