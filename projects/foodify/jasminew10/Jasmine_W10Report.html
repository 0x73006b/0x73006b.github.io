Project Direction

A) Topic

Our group chose to explore the Save-On-Foods Interface (sub-topic I) which allows a user to create a grocery list based on recipes or menus provided from a limited number of sources (e.g., excel spreadsheet, popular online recipe sites, built-in recipe database).

B) Requirements

Users can turn any inputted recipe into an ingredients list

  • Though this is a black box, being able to convert a recipe found online or from a recipe database to an ingredients list is the first step in creating a final grocery list.

Users can easily cross check ingredients they already have at home and ingredients they need to buy

  • Instead of the user doing calculations themselves on the grocery list to figure out the items they need to buy, this gives users a grocery list based on their logistical needs.

Users can find different brands and/or substitutes for ingredients and add it to their list 

  • (i.e. if a user doesn’t want to use tomatoes in their recipe they can substitute in red bell peppers )
  • This gives users a more personalized experience and allows for additional customization and flexibility.

Users are alerted of products with common food allergens

  • By taking into consideration the food allergies listed on the user’s profile, users can save time by not having to personally check the recipes for potential food allergens. This requirement allows for efficiency in the shopping process, as well as ensures users’ safety.

C) Task Examples

Task Example 1

Sam is a college student trying to learn how to cook. Recently, he’s been finding recipes by watching Youtube videos. Sometimes he finds it too tedious to pause and rewind the video each time he misses an ingredient or a step. He wants something that translates a video into a recipe, which will provide him with the list of ingredients needed for the dish. He then checks what ingredients he has at home and tries to cross-check it with the recipe in order to produce a grocery list. Additionally, because he is saving money,  Sam wants to compare prices between brands of the same product and put the cheaper option on his grocery list. He is also lactose intolerant, so he wants to easily find a substitution if the recipe calls for a dairy product, and add that to his grocery list instead. He then gets lazy and opts to have his groceries delivered to him.

Task Example 2

Martha is a single mother of 2. She wants a quick and easy way to turn recipes into a grocery list. Instead of spending a long time browsing for a recipe online, Martha wants a bunch of recipes ready for her to choose from. She wants to save the recipes that her and her children liked. However, she wants to be able to edit the grocery list once she selects on a saved or liked recipe, since the ingredients she has at home might differ from the previous time she went grocery shopping for the same recipe. Martha typically decides what to cook for dinner while on her lunch break at work. The grocery store is on her way home from work, so she wants to be able to order her groceries during lunch and then pick it up after she is done work. She usually makes dinner after she drops her kids off at their after school lessons on the same day.

Paper Prototyping and Cognitive Walkthrough Report

D) Brainstorming & Conceptual Design

Before splitting off into sub-groups, 3 overarching evaluation goals were decided upon to guide each prototype (Appendix A.1.0):

  1. Intuitive user-flow (How well can the user navigate through the application to complete a task?)
  2. High feature discoverability (How easy is it for the user to discover what affordances the interface allows?)
  3. Layout aesthetic (Are features laid out so they are aesthetically pleasing and visible?)

Both sub-groups used the 3 questions above to evaluate their prototype during the walkthrough. However, Subgroup 1 focused on automatically generating a grocery list from a given database of recipes (speed and ease), while Subgroup 2 focused on allowing the user to create a highly customized grocery list based off user-inputted recipes (customizability & personalization).

Subgroup 1 (Shabab, Simeon, Cody) — Foodify V1.0

This conceptual model (Appendix A.1.1) is most similar to the conceptual model of Spotify, where users have the option of either searching for a specific recipe, or “shuffling” to find something new.

On the home screen, users are given the choice of choosing a recipe from “Recent Tastes”, “Sales”, “Favorites”, or “Shuffling” (user is given a random recipe from the database). Alternatively, users can also use the Search button to search for a specific recipe in the case that they know exactly what meal they would like to prepare. Users can apply additional filters, and filter by price, meal type, and type of cuisine. After selecting a recipe, users can add to favorite if they like it, and are taken to a “Grocery List” containing all required ingredients and quantities of the recipe. Here, they can increase or decrease the quantity of items based on their knowledge of what they have at home. When they finalize the Grocery List, they add it to the shopping cart, where they can then either checkout or add more recipes/ingredients.

Subgroup 2 (Grace, Rithikha, Margaret) — Foodify V2.0 

This conceptual model (Appendix A.1.2) focuses on allowing the user to create a highly customized grocery list based off a user-inputted recipe. Over time, the user builds their own database of recipes through their previous app history and saved recipes.

When a user first logs in, they create a user profile where they can list personal information such as allergies and their address. This information is then used to alert the user of these ingredients when creating their final grocery list, as well as for delivery/identifying stores near them for pickup. Users can either input a new recipe or pick from previous recipes they’ve already converted. Once converted, users first identify which ingredients in the list they already have and in what amounts. The ingredients they don’t have then become the preliminary grocery list which users can further personalize (choose between brands, types, substitutes, quantity). The final grocery list is created once the user specifies each one of these parameters for each grocery list item. Users then checkout and specify their pick-up and payment options.

E) Paper Prototyping Rationale

Subgroup 1 (Shabab, Simeon, Cody) — Foodify V1.0

Step 1: We discussed ideas, taking ideas and working off of an affinity diagram created during a lab and previous discussions we’ve had (A.1.0). We also took inspiration from notable platforms that we judged to have good UI/UX (specifically Spotify).

Step 2: Our scope was contained to the essentials, covering only what we believed was required to complete a cognitive walkthrough of the scenario. For speed and flexibility we prototyped on Adobe XD (A.1.1, Image 1). XD allowed us to rearrange buttons and placements as needed, and change the screen flows as needed. These changes were made on the go as XD allows for live testing. We also decided on aesthetics and layout while making the XD mockup.

Step 3:  The paper prototype integrated some of the ideas from the XD mockup and talks; it was purposely made lower fidelity so we could concentrate on usability concerns.

Subgroup 2 (Grace, Rithikha, Margaret) — Foodify V2.0

Step 1: To prototype user-flow, our team first brainstormed and considered workflows by implementing an informal layout of the interface using Figma (A.1.2, Image 3). This layout consisted of simple black and grey boxes that represent a general idea on what high-level actions the user can perform. This allowed us to focus on what the user might need and how the user will move from subtask to subtask, without being distracted by aesthetic considerations.

Step 2: With the general user-flow in mind, we then began focusing on feature visibility and aesthetic considerations using a whiteboard to get all of our ideas out and see what layout design patterns, considerations, and priorities emerged as a group (A1.2, Image 4). We determined the scope and coverage of the prototype by narrowing down the essential tasks the user needs to perform and considered how to communicate these features to the user in a clear but aesthetically pleasing way. Using a whiteboard allowed us to get our ideas out fast and make quick changes as we went.

Step 3: For our final paper prototype (A1.2, Image 5), we integrated and polished up the whiteboard design ideas and user considerations we identified in our figma layout.

F) Cognitive Walkthrough Findings

Walkthrough video and steps can be found in Appendix A.2:

Subgroup 1 (Shabab, Cody, Simeon) — Foodify V1.0

This walkthrough was based off task example #2: Martha, a working, single mother of 2, quickly orders groceries during her lunch break so she can then pick them up after work and cook after dropping her kids off to after school lessons.

 

What Went Well:

  • Shuffle: The overall speed from getting from home to checkout is quite fast, I believe that can partially be attributed to the shuffle feature.
  • Intuitiveness: The user’s flow from screen to screen felt very sensible to us, the menus made sense.
  • Feature discoverability: The main features that people need to know about (for our prototype) are shuffle and favorites. Both were put up front on the homepage, and thus are highly discoverable

To Improve Upon:

  • Allergen alerts were not prompted: This is something that would be found in the actual description of the recipe, and there is a way to set allergens in the user profile (Appendix A.1.1 Image 2), however in a general screen flow, it’s something that should be brought up to ensure user safety.
  • There is a lack of alternative selection regarding ingredients: Because we focused quite a bit on ease of going from ‘A’ to ‘B’, we missed out on some levels of customizability that could’ve been added as an in-between screen
  • An interesting addition would’ve been schedule based delivery: Our task example is based around a single mother of 2, and thinking about it further, scheduling is likely to be a key aspect to manage time efficiently and currently there is no option to set pickup/delivery options at checkout.
  • Feedback: Issues were present regarding feedback; order confirmation is something that absolutely needs it, and as does allergen prompts (i.e. point 1).
  • Erroneous Signifier: The cuisine type selector uses radio buttons on the paper prototype, versus the XD mockup which uses iconography to represent cuisine types, due to an error in communication. This is minor, but important, issue that we need to fix for the future. See Appendix A.1.1’s ‘Meal Type’ screens.

Subgroup 2 (Grace, Rithikha, Margaret) — Foodify V2.0

This walkthrough was based off task example #1, where the user, a lactose-intolerant college student named Sam, is learning how to cook through Youtube videos and wants to get a corresponding list of grocery items that will be delivered to him.

What Went Well:

  • Despite some pop-ups that weren’t included, the general user flow (major steps of how a user goes from recipe to grocery list) is straight-forward and easy to understand, which was one of our evaluation goals.
  • There were lots of safety checks in between each step to ensure that the user hasn’t made a mistake (ie. having an extra screen that says “Mom’s Spaghetti” show up after a user has inserted a recipe ensures that it’s the recipe the user wants).
  • The interface was not too cluttered, making it easy for users to discover important features like “add new recipe” and there were many personalization options for each ingredient.

To Improve Upon:

  • Some details, such as a way to edit/add dietary restrictions or allergies, are missing.
  • It is unclear how to get rid of the ingredients that the user already has at home from the ingredients list (the swipe affordability does not have any signifier in the prototype).
  • Although the user already specified a dietary restriction, when a user chooses which type of ingredient to buy, they still have to press on the “show substitutes” button. It is not clearly flagged by prototype even though the interface has this information already. Furthermore this step may be redundant and may annoy users (efficiency is compromised by the high customizability).
  • There are also many nested screens (Each ingredient -> brand/type & price page -> substitutes page).
  • Need to add: A selection dropdown for dietary restrictions, a way to go back to the homepage on the screens & confirmation that an order has been received/payment has been processed once a user presses “Pay” in check-out.
